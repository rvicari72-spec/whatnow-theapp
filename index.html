Phase 1 — Stabilise: your first 30 days

Resist the urge to fire off applications. Those who stabilise first search better. This phase is about survival, structure, and protecting your identity before the search begins.

"The first thing most people do is wrong. They panic, update their CV overnight, and start applying everywhere. What they actually need is to stop, breathe, and get their house in order."

— Chapter 1: The First Week

    Phase 2 — Clarify: who you are and what you want

    The book calls this "the most skipped and most regretted phase." Most people spend months chasing roles that turn out to be wrong. Get clear before you get moving.

    "Clarity is not a luxury — it is a competitive advantage. The candidate who knows exactly what they want and why is ten times easier to place than the one who will consider anything."

    — Chapter 3: Getting Honest With Yourself

      Phase 3 — Position: craft your story

      Your story must work in three places simultaneously: on paper (CV), online (LinkedIn), and in person (verbal). Inconsistency between them destroys credibility instantly.

      "Recruiters are pattern-matchers. They read your CV in under ten seconds on first pass. If your headline doesn't immediately signal fit, they move on. Your job is to make the pattern obvious."

      — Chapter 4: Your Narrative Architecture

        Phase 4 — Search: working the market intelligently

        80% of senior roles are never advertised. Your search strategy must reflect market reality, not how you assume it works.

        "The people who spend all day on LinkedIn jobs and Indeed are working very hard and getting very little done. The people who have coffee are the ones getting hired."

        — Chapter 5: Finding the Invisible Market

          Phase 5 — Land: interviews, offers, decisions

          An offer is not the finish line. The book dedicates a full chapter to what most candidates get wrong between offer and signature.

          "Every single offer I have ever seen had room to negotiate. Not always on salary — but on something. The candidate who accepts in silence leaves money or terms on the table every time."

          — Chapter 8: Never Accept the First Number
