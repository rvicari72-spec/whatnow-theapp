The Why — your answer to "what happened?"

Three versions. Three audiences. The most important thing you will prepare.

› 📖

You will be asked this question more times than you expect. Your answer needs to be three things simultaneously: sharp, transparent, and calibrated to your audience. It should never be a rant. It should never be an excuse. And it should never be a performance of false peace you have not actually made.

To a senior executive or potential employer: keep it factual, clean, and forward-looking. Culture misalignment, shifting strategic priorities, investor pressure, internal politics — all are legitimate. One sentence on what happened. One sentence on what you learned. One sentence on where you are going. No bitterness, no excessive detail, no recruiting them as a sympathiser.

Your family — especially your children — need honest but calm language. Companies make business decisions. You may privately believe the decision was wrong or unfair. That is allowed. But the energy you spend on grievance is energy you cannot spend on recovery. Say it clearly: this is not the end of the story. We move forward.

The most important audience of all — be genuinely vulnerable. There are always two sides to every professional ending. What did you see coming and not address? Where did the relationship break down, and what was your part in it? Do not hold a grudge. Grudges are extraordinarily expensive. Then — let it go.

Objection handling

Prepare your answers to the six questions that will be asked, directly or indirectly, in every conversation.

› 📖

The book is clear: interviewers rarely voice their concerns directly. They ask around them. Your job is to anticipate the concern, reframe it as a strength, and make the answer feel natural — not defensive. Write your prepared response for each. Then practise delivering them aloud until the hesitation disappears.

Company size

The unstated concern: "You've only worked in large corporates — can you operate without the infrastructure?" or its reverse: "You've only done start-ups — can you navigate a complex organisation?"

Tenure

The unstated concern: "You've moved a lot — are you a flight risk?" or its reverse: "You've been at the same place for twenty years — can you adapt to somewhere new?"

Domain knowledge

The unstated concern: "You're too specialised — you won't see the bigger picture" or: "You're too much of a generalist — you don't know our business deeply enough."

Ageism & other -isms

The unstated concern: rarely explicit. Often expressed as "culture fit", "energy", "digital native", "fresh perspective". Also applies to gender, ethnicity, disability, background. These questions are illegal. The bias is real.

Academic background

The unstated concern: "They don't have an engineering background — can they really lead a tech company?" or: "Overly technical — will they understand the commercial side?"

Industry contacts

The unstated concern: "They're new to our sector — they won't know the right people, the right dynamics, or the unwritten rules." Particularly acute when switching industries.

Your five groups of contacts

Chapter 5: the exact framework for structuring your network campaign.

› 📖

The book organises your entire network into five groups — by what you need from them and what they can realistically give. Work through all five before you reach out to anyone. The temptation is to call Group 1 immediately. Resist it until you know what you are asking for.

Group 1 — People who will give you advice
These are people who have navigated similar territory — a job loss, a career pivot, a long search — and emerged. They are not necessarily your closest friends. They are the people whose judgement you trust on this specific subject. Their value is not their network. It is their hard-won perspective. Ask them: "What do you wish you had known?" Ask them to challenge your thinking, not to validate it. This group should be small — five to eight people at most. Choose carefully.
Group 2 — People who will make introductions
These are people with wide networks who know you well enough to vouch for you — and who move in circles that matter to your search. Former bosses, senior peers, board members you have worked alongside, investors who know your work. The ask here is specific: "Is there someone you think I should talk to?" or "Would you be willing to introduce me to X?" This group is the engine of the hidden market. Most senior roles are filled through a chain of introductions that starts here. Invest in these relationships before you need them.
Group 3 — People who might give you a job
These are hiring managers, CEOs, board chairs, and investors at organisations you have targeted. You may not know them well — or at all. This is the group most people try to reach first, and get wrong, because they arrive without context, without a warm introduction, and without clarity on what they are asking for. Do not contact this group until you have completed your positioning work, built your 60-prospect list, and secured at least some introductions through Group 2. When you do reach out, make the ask easy, make your relevance obvious, and make clear what you are looking for.
Group 4 — People who can help your career progression
This group is not about the immediate search. It is about the career beyond this search. Coaches, mentors, non-executive directors who have agreed to advise you, industry association chairs, academic connections, former professors or programme directors. These are people who can shape how you think about your career over the next decade — not just how you land the next role. Do not neglect this group because the immediate search feels urgent. The best time to build these relationships is now, when you have the space to think, not when you are fourteen months into a role and too busy to look up.
Group 5 — Headhunters and executive search
Headhunters sit in their own category because the relationship is fundamentally different from all the others. They do not represent you. They represent their client — the company that has paid them to find someone. Their job is to fill a role, not to manage your career. Understand this clearly and you will interact with them far more effectively. Your goal is not to register with as many firms as possible. It is to be genuinely known — as a person, not a CV — by five or six partners at firms that specialise in your function and level. Call them before you need them. Share useful intelligence. Engage with their content. Make the relationship two-directional. And when they do call with a role, even one that is not right for you, respond promptly and be genuinely helpful. Reputation in search circles travels faster than you think.

      The four stages of a senior search

      From the book: I just lost my (corporate leadership) job. What now? — each stage has its own logic, its own pace, and its own failure modes. Most people skip Stage 1 and pay for it throughout the rest of the search.

      1
      Reflection, research and clarity
      You cannot sell what you have not defined. The clarity stage is not preparation — it is the work.

      Most people skip this stage. They spend a day or two updating their CV, reach out to a handful of contacts, and consider themselves in the market. They are not. They are in motion without direction — which is arguably worse than standing still, because it burns through the goodwill of their network before they know what they are actually asking for. A focused candidate is a compelling candidate. A vague candidate is forgettable.

      Start with the evidence, not the aspiration. Look at the last three to five years of your career and identify the outcomes you produced — not the responsibilities you held, but the things that actually changed because you were there. Revenue grown. Deals closed. Products shipped. Teams built. Markets entered. That intersection — what you have demonstrably done, and what the market values — is your transferable value proposition.

      Get clear on five dimensions: role (specific title and seniority), vertical (which sectors), geography (where you will work), company size (startup to multinational — be honest about where you have thrived), and solution domain (the specific type of product or service). The narrower and more specific your domain definition, the more precisely it points to organisations where you are not just a good candidate — but the obvious one.

      Build a list of 50–60 target organisations. For each one, identify the specific hiring manager by name. When you ask for an introduction, you want to say: I am looking to connect with the Chief Revenue Officer at Company X — do you know them? Not: do you know anyone at Company X?

      "A focused candidate is a compelling one. A vague candidate is a forgettable one."
      ✓ Do
      • ✓Identify transferable outcomes — not just responsibilities
      • ✓Define solution domain, company size, role, vertical, and geography specifically
      • ✓Build a named list of 50–60 target companies
      • ✓Identify the specific hiring managers by name
      • ✓Rehearse your pitch out loud, on camera, with feedback
      ✗ Don't
      • ✗Start outreach before your clarity work is done
      • ✗Describe yourself in broad, generic terms
      • ✗Send the same message to every contact
      • ✗Rely on headhunters and job boards as your primary channel
      • ✗Skip rehearsal because you think you will wing it well
      2
      Outbound search: the five groups
      Not everyone in your network can help you in the same way. The mistake is treating them as if they can.

      A structured outbound search starts by recognising that your contacts fall into fundamentally different categories — each requiring a different approach, a different ask, and a different investment of your time and theirs.

      "Your network is not a single resource. It is five different ones. Use each one accordingly."
      Group How to approach them
      Group 1
      Give you advice      		Talk to them early. They help you sharpen your story and stress-test your target list. Do not ask them for introductions they cannot credibly make — it wastes their goodwill and produces weak referrals.
      Group 2
      Make an introduction      		These contacts are connectors. For these conversations to produce results, you must be completely clear about who you want to meet. "Someone in fintech" produces nothing. "The CRO at a Series B payments company" produces a call.
      Group 3
      Potentially give you a job      		Your highest-priority group. Do not open with the ask — open with genuine curiosity about their business and challenges. Approach them as a peer, not as a supplicant. The ask follows naturally from a conversation in which you have demonstrated relevance.
      Group 4
      Offer career progression      		Contacts at companies representing not just your next step but a meaningful step up — a larger remit, a more strategic platform, a bigger market. They require your most polished pitch and clearest articulation of how you specifically add value.
      Group 5
      Headhunters      		Headhunters work for companies, not candidates. Register with the right ones for your sector, keep them informed of your situation, and treat them as one channel among several — not your primary one.

      Sequencing: start with Groups 1 and 2, where pressure is lower. Move to Groups 3 and 4 once your pitch is sharp. Approach headhunters early — mandates move quickly and you want to already be on their radar. Track every conversation with a next step and a follow-up date.

      "The ask must match the group. Asking Group 1 for a job is awkward. Asking Group 3 for generic advice is a missed opportunity."
      ✓ Do
      • ✓Map your contacts to the five groups before reaching out
      • ✓Be specific about who you want to meet — not vaguely curious
      • ✓Approach Group 3 and 4 contacts as a peer, with prepared relevance
      • ✓Track every conversation with a next step and follow-up date
      • ✓Register with the right headhunters for your sector early
      ✗ Don't
      • ✗Treat your network as one undifferentiated resource
      • ✗Ask people to help without telling them specifically how
      • ✗Open a Group 3 conversation with the ask before building context
      • ✗Rely on headhunters as your primary or only channel
      • ✗Let conversations go without a clear next step
      3
      Radio silence: how not to let desperation take over
      It will go quiet. This is normal. What you do in the silence is what determines whether the opportunity survives.

      Every senior job search has periods of silence. A conversation that seemed to be building momentum suddenly stops. A company that was enthusiastic goes quiet for two weeks. These silences feel personal. In the vast majority of cases, they are none of these things. They are process. Hiring committees reschedule. Decision-makers travel. Internal priorities shift for reasons that have nothing to do with you.

      "Silence is not rejection. It is the buying cycle doing what buying cycles do."

      When candidates become anxious, they follow up too frequently, lower their bar, and say yes to conversations they should decline — signalling to the market that they are available rather than exceptional. Seniority and availability are inversely correlated in most people's minds. The candidate who appears to have options is more attractive than the one who appears to be waiting.

      The antidote is structure, not willpower. Follow up once, after a reasonable interval, with something of value to add. Not "just checking in" — but "I came across this piece on the topic we discussed." Maintain your own pipeline so that no single opportunity carries all the weight. When five conversations are in motion, silence from one is annoying. When only one conversation is in motion, silence from it is devastating.

      At the end of every week, rank your active opportunities in order of probability and speed to convert. Strike out the ones that should be qualified out based on sustained lack of response. A clean list of five real conversations is worth more than a bloated list of twenty going nowhere.

      "The candidate who appears to have options is more attractive than the one who appears to be waiting. Manage the perception — and manage yourself."
      ✓ Do
      • ✓Follow up once, with something of value to add
      • ✓Keep the pipeline full — silence from one hurts less
      • ✓Use quiet periods for refinement: pitch, list, research
      • ✓Maintain your physical routine and mental equilibrium
      • ✓Remember: silence is process, not judgement
      ✗ Don't
      • ✗Follow up more than once in two weeks on the same thread
      • ✗Widen your search indiscriminately when things go quiet
      • ✗Say yes to conversations you should decline
      • ✗Signal availability when you should signal selectivity
      • ✗Let a single stalled opportunity consume your emotional energy
      4
      One or more companies have chosen you. Now what?
      Being the preferred candidate is not the finish line. It is the moment most people mishandle.

      At some point — if you have executed Stages 1, 2, and 3 well — one or more companies will identify you as their preferred candidate. The errors come in two directions: either the candidate becomes too passive, assuming the deal is done, or they become too aggressive, pressing hard on terms before the relationship is fully secured. Both approaches can cost you the offer. What is needed is active patience — engaged, confident, and disciplined.

      "Being their top choice means they want you. It does not mean you have the job. Keep going."

      The preferred candidate position gives you leverage — but leverage only holds if the other party believes you have alternatives. If you have been running a proper pipeline search, this should be true. Continue to be genuinely engaged and responsive. If you have other live processes, be transparent without being aggressive: "I am in discussions with a couple of other organisations and I would like to be respectful of everyone's time" is a legitimate and useful thing to say.

      Negotiation is most effective when framed as a conversation about fit rather than a battle over terms. Know your number before the conversation. Know which elements matter most to you — base salary, bonus, equity, title, reporting line, flexibility — and rank them. Not everything can be negotiated, and some things matter more than others.

      Respond to an offer within a reasonable timeframe. Asking for a week is normal. Asking for three weeks signals indecision. When the offer comes and the terms are right, accept it clearly and enthusiastically. The moment of acceptance is the foundation of the new professional relationship.

      "A short honest list beats a long comfortable one. Every time."
      ✓ Do
      • ✓Keep your pipeline running until a contract is signed
      • ✓Be transparent about other processes without being aggressive
      • ✓Know your priorities before the negotiation conversation begins
      • ✓Respond to offers promptly and decisively
      • ✓Accept clearly and enthusiastically when the terms are right
      ✗ Don't
      • ✗Assume the deal is done before it is signed
      • ✗Invent competing offers you do not have
      • ✗Negotiate every term with equal intensity
      • ✗Delay responding without a clear and honest reason
      • ✗Let the negotiation damage the relationship you have built
