I just lost my (corporate leadership) job.
What now?

The first hours after the news. What you are feeling is real — and what you do next matters more than you think.

It might have come as a complete shock. Or perhaps there were signs — a restructuring quietly underway, a relationship with your manager that had cooled, a reorg that seemed to leave your function oddly exposed, a strategy meeting you were strangely not invited to attend.

Either way, the moment it becomes real — the call, the meeting, the carefully worded email — something shifts. The ground you have been standing on turns out to have been less solid than you thought.

Senior leaders are not immune to this feeling. In fact, the more senior you are, the more disorienting the experience can be. Your identity, your calendar, your daily purpose, your status — all of it has been bound up in that role for years. Losing the role does not just mean losing income. It can feel like losing yourself.

"The instinct is to act immediately. The wisdom is to pause long enough to act well."

This app will provide an instrument to help navigate the next three to four months without making the mistakes that delay or derail what is, for most people in your position, an inevitable recovery.

The uncomfortable truth is this: you are not in control of how quickly the right opportunity arrives. You cannot manufacture a job offer. You cannot speed up a hiring committee. What you can do is protect your candidacy, manage your energy, and avoid the errors that are entirely within your control to prevent.

Your five groups of contacts

The book's framework for mapping your network before you start reaching out.

›

Before reaching out to anyone, map your full network into these five groups. The book is explicit: most people skip straight to Group 1 and ignore Groups 3–5, which is where most senior opportunities actually originate.

Group 1 — Close allies
People who know you well, trust you completely, and will actively advocate for you. Former bosses, long-term colleagues, close professional friends.
Group 2 — Warm network
People you know reasonably well but haven't spoken to recently. Former peers, clients, stakeholders. They'd take your call. They may not think of you proactively.
Group 3 — Weak ties
People you've met once or twice, connected with on LinkedIn, or know by reputation. Research shows this is where most unexpected senior opportunities originate.
Group 4 — Executive search
Headhunters and search firm partners — those you have worked with before, and those you need to introduce yourself to. Treat as a structured campaign, not a passive registration.
Group 5 — Connectors & introducers
People who may not hire you themselves but know everyone. Investors, advisors, NEDs, industry association chairs, journalists, lawyers. One introduction from this group can unlock multiple doors simultaneously.
